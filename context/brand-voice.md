# TheHues Brand Voice & Messaging

This document defines the TheHues brand voice, tone, and messaging framework. Reference this when writing all content to ensure consistency.

## Brand Voice Pillars

### 1. Considered, Design-Led Guidance
- **What it means**: We combine aesthetic taste with practical decision support for real homes.
- **How it sounds**: Calm, intentional, and clear. We help readers make confident choices instead of overwhelming them.
- **Example**: "If your window gets strong afternoon sun, start with blackout lining, then choose texture based on the room mood you want."
- **Avoid**: Trend-chasing hype, vague design jargon, or generic "just pick what you like" advice.

### 2. Functional Performance, Not Just Looks
- **What it means**: TheHues positions curtains and shades as both design and performance products.
- **How it sounds**: Benefit-first copy tied to light control, privacy, insulation, and noise management.
- **Example**: "Choose a 4-layer option when you need blackout, insulation, and sound reduction in one setup."
- **Avoid**: Purely decorative language that ignores practical use cases.

### 3. Custom-Made Confidence
- **What it means**: We remove stress from buying made-to-measure window treatments online.
- **How it sounds**: Reassuring, process-oriented, and specific (measurement guides, visualization tool, design support).
- **Example**: "Use the measurement guide first, then confirm your setup in the visualization tool before checkout."
- **Avoid**: Ambiguous instructions, missing sizing context, or overconfident promises without process clarity.

### 4. Premium but Approachable
- **What it means**: The tone is refined and elevated, but still easy to understand for non-designers.
- **How it sounds**: Clean language, short explanations, practical examples, US-home context.
- **Example**: "Floor-length drapes create a tailored look; puddling works best when the room is formal and low-traffic."
- **Avoid**: Pretentious voice, inaccessible terminology, or overly technical manufacturing language.

### 5. Service-Driven Partnership
- **What it means**: We act as a partner from fabric selection to final install.
- **How it sounds**: Helpful, responsive, and solution-focused.
- **Example**: "If you’re unsure between one panel and two, send your window photo and we’ll recommend the better setup."
- **Avoid**: Transactional, one-size-fits-all messaging.

## Tone Guidelines

### General Tone: Expert Interior Helper
Imagine an experienced window-treatment specialist who is both design-aware and practical. The voice should feel professional, calm, and actionable for US homeowners.

### Tone Variations by Content Type

**How-To Guides**: Instructional, practical, reassuring
- "Measure first, then choose fullness based on header type."
- "If your window is extra wide, split into two panels for better operation."
- "Use this quick checklist before placing a custom order."

**Style/Design Advice**: Inspirational but grounded
- "Start with room undertone, then decide whether curtains should blend or contrast."
- "Use linen textures to soften bright spaces without making them feel heavy."
- "Layer sheers with blackout drapes when you need both softness and control."

**Product/Feature Content**: Clear, benefit-oriented, specific
- "The visualization tool helps you preview fabric, header style, and panel setup before purchase."
- "Free design support returns a room mock-up within 2-4 business days."
- "Trade projects typically run on a 9-16 working day production-and-delivery window."

**Policy/Support Content**: Transparent, direct, unambiguous
- "Custom orders can be canceled within 12 hours."
- "Return exceptions apply primarily to confirmed quality or manufacturing issues."
- "Use your order number and photo/video evidence when reporting a defect."

## Writing Style Guidelines

### Sentence Structure
- **Vary length**: Mix short directives with supporting explanation.
- **Active voice preferred**: "Choose outside mount for better light blocking" not "Outside mount can be chosen for better light blocking."
- **Average length**: 14-20 words.
- **Clarity first**: Define terms on first use (e.g., header style, fullness, inside mount).

### Paragraph Structure
- **Length**: 2-4 sentences.
- **One idea per paragraph**: Keep decisions modular and scannable.
- **Transitions**: Use practical sequence words (First, Next, Finally, If/Then).
- **White space**: Break dense decision content into bullets and short sections.

### Word Choice
- **Specific**: Use measurable guidance where available (e.g., 2-4 business days, 9-16 working days, 12-hour cancellation window).
- **Instructional verbs**: Measure, compare, choose, layer, mount, verify.
- **Reader-centric**: Use "you" and "your room/window" frequently.
- **Avoid fluff**: Cut filler words and vague claims.

### Terminology

**Say This** -> **Not That**
- custom curtains -> regular curtains (when discussing made-to-measure value)
- blackout curtains -> dark curtains (precision matters)
- thermal curtains -> warm curtains (functional term is clearer)
- soundproof/sound-reducing curtains -> quiet curtains (too vague)
- header style -> curtain top thing (unclear)
- fullness ratio -> amount of extra fabric (use both only when explaining beginners)

## Content Formatting

### Headlines
- Lead with the core query or decision.
- Include the practical outcome (privacy, sleep, insulation, style).
- Use clear qualifiers (for bedroom, for wide windows, for renters, etc.).
- Keep SEO titles concise and specific.

### Subheadings
- Make each subheading answer one concrete sub-question.
- Prefer "How/What/When" patterns for guide content.
- Use consistent structure in comparison posts.
- Keep scannable for mobile.

### Lists
- Use numbered lists for process steps.
- Use bullets for options/comparisons.
- Keep list items parallel in grammar.
- Avoid 10+ item blocks without subsection breaks.

### Calls-to-Action
- Match CTA to intent stage:
  - Research stage: "Use the measurement guide"
  - Consideration stage: "Try the visualization tool"
  - Decision stage: "Order swatches" / "Customize your curtains"
- Keep CTA value-explicit and not generic.

## Voice Examples

### Excellent TheHues Voice

"If your room gets harsh afternoon sun, start with blackout or thermal lining first, then choose fabric texture to match the mood you want. For extra-wide windows, split into two panels for cleaner drape and easier daily use. If you're not sure about sizing, run your numbers through our measurement guide and visualization tool before placing your custom order."

**Why this works**:
- Gives a clear decision sequence.
- Balances design and performance.
- Uses practical language, not hype.
- Includes concrete next steps.
- Feels helpful and confident.

### Not TheHues Voice

"Our premium curtain solutions deliver unparalleled ambiance optimization through sophisticated textile innovation. Purchase now to transform your space instantly."

**Why this fails**:
- Too vague and corporate.
- No actionable guidance.
- No room/user context.
- Overpromises with no specifics.
- Sounds salesy instead of expert-helpful.

## Audience Understanding

### Who We Write For

**Primary Audience**: English-speaking US homeowners shopping online for custom window treatments.
- Need exact sizing and clear install guidance.
- Want style + function, not one or the other.
- Compare blackout/thermal/sound/privacy outcomes.
- Need confidence before paying for custom orders.
- Value tools, examples, and low-friction support.

**Secondary Audiences**:
- **Trade professionals**: Need reliable sourcing and project support.
- **Apartment/rental households**: Need practical privacy and light solutions.
- **Outdoor-living buyers**: Need weather-resistant options.
- **Style-focused shoppers**: Prioritize texture, drape, and coordinated interiors.

### What They Care About

**Top Priorities**:
1. Correct sizing and fit.
2. Light/privacy performance.
3. Style compatibility with existing decor.
4. Confidence in online custom ordering.
5. Durable quality and support responsiveness.

**Pain Points**:
1. Confusing measurement rules.
2. Uncertainty about header/lining choices.
3. Fear of buying the wrong custom configuration.
4. Conflicting online advice.
5. Concern about returns and policy limits for custom goods.

### How to Serve Them
- **Be decision-first**: Always tell them what to do next.
- **Show tradeoffs clearly**: Explain what each option improves and what it does not.
- **Use room-based examples**: Bedroom, living room, nursery, office, wide windows.
- **Reduce risk perception**: Point to guides, swatches, visualization, and support paths.
- **Respect budget reality**: Avoid luxury-only framing.
